Climbing Glossary

Anchor: A point of attachment for a climber's rope, typically consisting of bolts, trees, or other natural features.

Barn door: A term for when the climber's body swings uncontrollably away from the rock, often due to all points of contact being in line with each other.

Belay: The technique of managing the rope for a climber, providing security and arresting falls.

Belayer: The person who manages the rope and provides a belay for the climber.

Belaying device: A mechanical device used to control the rope while belaying, such as an ATC or GriGri.

Beta: Information or advice about how to complete a climb or a specific move.

Bouldering: Climbing short, challenging routes called "problems" without ropes, but with a crashpad for protection.

Cams: Spring-loaded devices placed in cracks to protect climbers, used in trad climbing.

Carabiner: A metal loop with a spring-loaded gate used to connect climbing gear, ropes, and harnesses.

Chalk: Powder used by climbers to keep their hands dry for better grip.

Chimney: A wide crack that is climbed by placing the body inside and using opposite pressure from the feet and back.

Clipstick: An extendable stick with a mechanism for clipping a quickdraw and rope to a distant bolt.

Clipping: The act of attaching the rope to a piece of protection (e.g. a quickdraw) to secure yourself on the climb.

Crag: A cliff or group of cliffs that are suitable for climbing.

Crashpad: A thick, portable foam pad used to cushion falls in bouldering.

Crimp: A small hold/edge that you grip with your fingertips, often with bent fingers.

Crux: The hardest move or sequence of moves on a climb.

Dyno: A dynamic move where you jump or lunge to reach the next hold.

DWS (Deep water soloing): Climbing over water without ropes, relying on the water to catch you if you fall.

Edging: Using the edge of the climbing shoe to stand on small footholds.

FA / First ascent: The first ascent of a route/problem.

Flagging: A technique where one leg is extended out to the side to improve balance.

Flash: Climbing a route on the first try but with some prior knowledge or advice about the route.

Grading: An alphanumeric system used to indicate the difficulty of a problem/route (see Font).

Gaston: A move where you push outward against a hold with your elbow pointing away from your body.

Font (Fontainebleau) grading: A grading system named after Fontainebleau in France (e.g. bouldering 4, 6B, 7C+; sport/trad 4, 6b, 7c+).

Hand jam: A technique where the hand is wedged into a crack to provide a secure hold.

Heel hook: Using the heel to hook onto a hold to provide stability or to pull up.

Helmet: Always, ALWAYS, wear one on a sport/trad route! Protective headgear worn to prevent injuries from falls or rockfall.

Hype: Excitement and anticipation among climbers, often surrounding new crags, climbs, or events.

Jug: A large, easy-to-grip hold, often very secure and comfortable.

Layback: A climbing technique where you pull on a vertical edge with your hands while pushing with your feet.

Leader: A person who climbs first without a rope running through an anchor point above, clipping into attachment points as they progress.

Lock off: Holding a position with one arm while reaching for the next hold with the other.

Mantle: A move where the climber pushes down on a hold to bring their body up and over.

Onsight: Climbing a route on the first try without any prior knowledge or advice about the route.

Overhang: A section of the climb that leans past vertical, making it more physically demanding.

Pinch: A type of hold that is gripped by pinching between the thumb and fingers.

Pitch: A section of a climb between two belay points.

Problem: A boulder route (see "bouldering)."

Pumped: When your forearms are so fatigued and filled with lactic acid that they can hardly grip the holds.

Quickdraw: A piece of climbing gear used to connect the rope to bolts or other protection, consisting of two carabiners connected by a sling.

Redpoint: Successfully climbing a route after having practiced or attempted it previously.

Roof: A completely horizontal overhang, often requiring complex and powerful moves.

Send: To successfully climb a route or problem. (For the purposes of this app, "send" is any send type that is not an onsight or a flash.)

Slab: A less-than-vertical face that relies more on balance and footwork than upper body strength.

Sloper: A hold that is smooth and rounded, relying on friction rather than edges.

Sport climbing: Climbing routes that are pre-equipped with fixed bolts and anchors for protection.

Spotter: A person who helps to protect a climber from the ground in bouldering by guiding their fall towards the crashpad.

Tick-list: A list of climbs a climber wants to complete.

Topo: A diagram or map of a climbing area or route showing the features, holds, and protection points.

Trad climbing: Traditional climbing where the climber places their own protective gear, like cams and nuts, into cracks and features in the rock.
